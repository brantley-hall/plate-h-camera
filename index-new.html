The Lost Daguerreotype of Washington Square

In the spring of 1839, as the new art of photography spread across America, Professor John William Draper stood on the rooftop of NYU's Gothic Revival building on Washington Square. With his experimental daguerreotype camera, he captured what would become one of the earliest photographic images of New York City.

Among Draper's early experiments was a mysterious image known only as "Plate H" - a sixth-plate daguerreotype that captured the nascent university building and the surrounding streets of Greenwich Village. This remarkable image, preserved through the research of Howard R. McManus of The History Broker, offers us a rare glimpse into New York's photographic dawn.

Through careful analysis of Plate H and the historical record, we can reconstruct not just what Draper saw through his lens in 1839, but understand the technical innovations that made this image possible, and the urban landscape that surrounded one of America's first photographic laboratories.
