Plate H Daguerreotype Collection

The complete collection of Plate H daguerreotypes and related photographic materials from John William Draper's early experiments at NYU, 1839-1840.

Technical Analysis of Plate H

Plate H represents one of the earliest surviving daguerreotypes taken in America, created by Professor John William Draper in the spring of 1839 from the rooftop of NYU's Gothic Revival building on Washington Square. This sixth-plate daguerreotype (approximately 2.75 x 3.25 inches) captures the nascent university building and the surrounding streets of Greenwich Village.

Technical Specifications

  • Format: Sixth-plate daguerreotype (2.75" x 3.25")
  • Date: Spring 1839 (circa March-May)
  • Location: NYU rooftop, Washington Square, New York
  • Photographer: Professor John William Draper
  • Lens System: Non-achromatic biconvex lens, 5-inch diameter, 7-inch focal length
  • Exposure Time: Estimated 5-8 minutes
  • Process: Direct daguerreotype on silver-plated copper

Historical Significance

Plate H is historically significant as one of the first architectural photographs taken in America. Draper's work predates most other American daguerreotypes and represents the intersection of scientific experimentation and artistic documentation. The image provides a rare glimpse into New York's urban landscape during the early years of photography, capturing the Gothic Revival building that housed one of America's first photographic laboratories.

Technical Innovations

Draper's approach to Plate H demonstrated several technical innovations that were crucial for early American photography:

  • Use of non-achromatic lenses with violet light focus for improved image quality
  • Chemical optimization for faster exposure times
  • Rooftop positioning for optimal lighting and perspective
  • Architectural subject matter requiring precise composition

Image Analysis

The Plate H daguerreotype shows remarkable technical quality for its era. The image captures the NYU building's Gothic architectural details with reasonable clarity, suggesting Draper had achieved significant control over the daguerreotype process. The composition demonstrates an understanding of perspective and architectural photography that would influence subsequent photographic documentation of American buildings.
